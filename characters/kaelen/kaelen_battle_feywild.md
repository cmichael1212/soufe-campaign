# The Serpent Grove

*Year Three of the wandering. Late autumn, the Feywild — the deep forest east of the Far Forest crossing.*

*First person. Kaelen.*

---

I smelled the poison before I saw it.

Not venom — poison. There's a difference. Venom is alive. It stinks of purpose, of intent, of something that evolved over millennia to say *stay away* or *you're already dead*. Poison is quieter. Poison seeps. It gets into the water and the soil and the roots and by the time you notice it the damage is three days old and spreading.

This was in the water. A creek I'd crossed a hundred times — cold, fast, tasting of snowmelt and granite — and today it tasted like copper and something chemical I couldn't name. The stones at the bottom had a film on them. Iridescent. Wrong.

I followed it upstream.

---

I knew the grove before I reached it. Everyone in the deep forest knew Lirael's grove.

She wasn't a dryad — people got that wrong. She was an eladrin, young by their count, and she'd chosen this place the way some eladrin choose a season. The grove was hers because she tended it, not because she was bound to it. Birch and rowan and a single ancient yew at the center that she talked to like a friend. She grew things — medicinal moss, moonpetal, silverbark lichen that could close a wound faster than any spell I'd seen. The satyrs came to her when they were hurt. The pixies brought her things — shiny stones, lost buttons, once an entire boot — and she thanked them for every one.

I knew her the way I knew most people in the deep forest. From a distance. A nod across a clearing. A bundle of fireweed left at the edge of her grove when I passed through, because she used it in her poultices and I knew where it grew thick. Once, during a bad frost, she'd found me sheltering under an overhang and brought me a tea that tasted like warm dirt and sunshine and made me sleep for fourteen hours straight. I woke up angry about it. She thought that was hilarious.

She had a laugh like water over stones. I'm telling you that now because I won't be able to tell you later.

---

I heard them before I saw the grove.

Voices. Not Sylvan, not Elvish, not any language the forest speaks. A hissing, clicking cadence that pressed against my ears like fingers on a bruise. And underneath the voices, a sound I knew in my bones — the wet, rhythmic thud of something being harvested. Cut. Taken.

I came through the treeline low and fast and what I saw made the rage jump straight past simmer into something white and wordless.

Six of them. Yuan-Ti. The purebloods were easy to miss if you didn't know what to look for — human enough at a glance, but the eyes were wrong, and the way they moved was wrong, all sideways efficiency like something that had learned to walk upright but didn't believe in it. Two of them were filling glass vessels from the creek with gloved hands, siphoning the tainted water into containers etched with sigils I didn't recognize. Two more were stripping the silverbark lichen from the rowan trees — not carefully, not the way Lirael did it, leaving enough for the tree to recover. They were peeling it off in sheets, killing the bark underneath, and the trees were screaming in the slow silent way that trees scream, and nobody was listening.

The fifth was a halfblood. Taller. Scales visible along the jaw and forearms, eyes like a snake's, vertical pupils that caught the filtered light and threw it back flat and gold. He carried a curved blade and a satchel that moved. Something alive was in that satchel and it was not happy about it.

The sixth was different.

He stood apart from the others, near the yew at the center of the grove. Not working. Watching. He was dressed better than the rest — dark leather, well-fitted, with a medallion at his throat that pulsed with a faint sickly green. A malison — more snake than human, the scales running up his neck and across half his face, one arm ending not in a hand but in a flat, wide head with its own set of eyes. He moved with the careful stillness of someone who was used to being the most dangerous thing in any room.

He was studying the yew tree the way a butcher studies a carcass.

And Lirael was on the ground.

---

She was alive. I could see her breathing — shallow, wrong, her autumn-red hair spread across the moss like something spilled. Her hands were bound behind her with cord that glowed faintly. She'd been hit. The left side of her face was swelling and there was blood at her temple, bright against her pale skin.

She'd fought. I could see that too. Scorch marks on the ground near her feet — she'd thrown fire, or tried to. One of the purebloods had a burn across his forearm that he was pointedly not acknowledging. But six against one, and her magic was the healing kind, the growing kind. She'd never been a fighter.

The one with the medallion — the malison — said something to her in that hissing language. She didn't respond. He crouched beside her and put the snake-hand near her face, and the little head on the end of his arm swayed back and forth, tasting the air around her.

I stopped thinking.

---

I want to tell you I had a plan. That I assessed the terrain and identified the threats and chose my moment with the cold precision of a seasoned ranger. I want to tell you the rage was a tool, controlled, directed, the way Yewshade taught me.

I can't tell you that.

What I can tell you is that the axe was in my hands and I was moving and the first pureblood died without knowing I was there. The greataxe took him across the back as he knelt by the creek, and the glass vessel in his hands shattered and the tainted water splashed across the stones and he folded and I was already past him.

The second one at the creek spun and got his blade up. I'll give him that. He blocked the first swing and the impact jarred my arms to the shoulders and I felt the rage catch like kindling — not hot, not yet, but close, building, the world going sharp at the edges the way it does when the blood knows something the mind hasn't processed.

I didn't block his counter. I stepped into it. The blade caught my forearm — I felt the bite, felt the skin open, felt the heat of it — and I hit him with my shoulder. Eight hundred pounds of Firbolg at full stride. He went backward into a rowan trunk and the sound his ribs made was like green wood splitting.

I was in the grove now. The lichen-strippers had dropped their tools and drawn short swords and they came at me together, which was smart, but they came from the front, which wasn't. I swung the axe in a flat arc — not aiming, just *clearing* — and one of them ducked and the other caught the haft across his chest and staggered.

The one who ducked stabbed upward. I felt it go into my thigh, a white spike of pain that the rage swallowed like a river swallows a stone. I grabbed his wrist with my free hand and *squeezed* and the bones in there made sounds that bones are not supposed to make and he screamed in a voice that was half hiss and I threw him.

Threw him into the halfblood, who was coming fast with the curved blade, and they tangled and went down and I had a breath, half a breath, and I used it to look at the malison.

He hadn't moved.

He was watching me the way a hawk watches a rabbit — except the calculation in his eyes said he wasn't sure yet which of us was which. Assessing. The snake-hand at his side was rigid, both sets of eyes — his and the arm's — fixed on me with an intensity that had nothing to do with fear and everything to do with calculation.

"Interesting," he said. Common tongue, accented, the sibilants drawn long. "The Connifer. We'd heard stories."

I didn't answer. My thigh was bleeding. My forearm was bleeding. The rage was up now, fully lit, roaring in my chest like a furnace with the damper pulled, and the edges of my vision were going gold — not the gold of the visions, not the gold of rooftops and broken sky, but the deep amber gold of sap, of heartwood, of something growing so fast and so fierce that it burns from the inside.

The World Tree. That's what it felt like. Roots in my feet reaching down through the soil of the Feywild and finding something vast underneath, something connected to every forest on every plane, and drawing up from it a strength that was not mine but was being lent to me by something ancient that wanted these snakes out of its garden.

"Kill him," the malison said, without inflection.

The halfblood and the last standing pureblood came at me.

---

What happened next — I remember it in pieces. Not because I was unconscious. Because the rage does that. It takes the neat linear thread of experience and smashes it into shards, and afterward you hold them up to the light and try to reassemble something that looks like a sequence of events and mostly fail.

I remember the halfblood's curved blade catching the light as he swung. I remember the sound my axe made when it hit his — a deep, bell-tone clang that shook the leaves from the nearest birch.

I remember the pureblood circling behind me. I remember knowing he was there without seeing him, the way you know where a tree's roots run without digging, and I remember spinning and catching him with the axe haft across the throat and the way his eyes went wide with a surprise that was almost human.

I remember the halfblood cutting me across the ribs. Deep. The pain was somewhere else — I could see it, like a light on the horizon, but it couldn't reach me through the rage. I hit him. Not with the axe. With my fist. My hand, the size of his entire head, and the impact traveled up my arm and into my shoulder and he went down and didn't get up.

I remember turning to the malison and he was gone.

Not fled. Gone. The space where he'd been standing was empty, and the air there smelled like ozone and snake and something chemical, and there was a fading shimmer in the shape of a doorway that hadn't been there before.

He'd had an exit planned. The whole time.

---

I went to Lirael.

The rage was still burning. I could feel it in my hands as I knelt beside her, in the tremor that made my fingers clumsy as I worked at the binding cord. The cord resisted — enchanted, woven with something that bit at my skin — and I snarled at it, actually snarled, like an animal, and tore it apart with my fingers because the axe was too big and too bloody for this and I couldn't find my knife.

Her breathing was wrong. I'd known that from across the grove but up close it was worse. Shallow. Wet. A sound in her chest that didn't belong there, a rattling that spoke of fluid where there shouldn't be fluid.

"Lirael."

Her eyes opened. Autumn amber, the color of the grove at its best, but the light in them was thin. Fading. She looked at me and I watched her try to focus and fail and try again.

"Connifer." Half a whisper. A ghost of a smile. "You're bleeding everywhere."

"You're worse."

"I know." She coughed, and the sound was terrible, and there was blood on her lips. "The tall one. The one with the arm. He — there was something in the blade. Not just the cut. Something in it."

Poison. Of course. The curved blade the malison had carried. He hadn't fought me. He hadn't needed to. He'd already done what he came to do. The harvest was a bonus. Lirael was the message.

"I can carry you. The elder grove is half a day west — the healers —"

"Connifer." Her hand found mine. Her fingers were cold. "No."

"Don't —"

"It's in my blood. I can feel it. The root kind — the kind that grows." She swallowed, and I could see what that cost her. "It's already in the yew. He put it in the yew first, and then in me. Same poison. Whatever they wanted from this place, they poisoned it so nobody else could have it."

I looked at the yew tree. The great ancient yew at the center of her grove, the one she talked to like a friend. Its bark was weeping a dark sap I'd never seen before. The leaves on its lowest branches were curling inward, edges blackening.

"You have to burn it," Lirael said.

The word hit me like a slap.

"The yew. The grove. If the poison reaches the root system it'll spread to every tree connected to it. A hundred trees. More. You have to burn the grove and salt the earth." She coughed again. Her grip on my hand tightened. "You have to."

I couldn't speak. The rage was gone — not banked, not controlled, just gone, replaced by something colder and heavier that sat in my chest like a stone.

"I know," she said. She knew. She'd seen me flinch from campfires. She'd seen me build them with the double ring and the damp earth and the waterskin. She knew what she was asking. "I know what fire means to you. I'm sorry."

"Don't apologize to me. You're the one who's —"

"Connifer." The smile again, fainter. "It's just a grove. I'll find another one. Somewhere with more sunlight. I've always wanted more sunlight."

She was lying. We both knew she was lying. The poison was in her blood and it was the root kind, the kind that grows, and there were no healers within half a day who could outrun what the malison had put in that blade.

I held her hand while the light in her eyes went out. It took longer than I wanted it to and not as long as it should have. The grove was quiet except for the sick drip of dark sap from the yew and the sound of the poisoned creek and, somewhere far away, a bird that didn't know what was happening here and sang anyway.

---

I burned the grove at dusk.

I built the fire the way I always do. Stones in a double ring. Damp earth in the gap. Waterskin within reach.

Then I lit the yew and stood back and watched Lirael's world burn.

The fire took the yew first, then the rowan, then the birch, spreading through the canopy the way she'd described the poison spreading through roots — fast, hungry, indiscriminate. The silverbark lichen popped and hissed. The moonpetal flared bright white before it turned to ash. The moss on the stones — the medicinal moss she'd cultivated for years — curled and blackened and was gone.

I stood in the heat with the warding ring dulling the bite against my skin and I watched until there was nothing left to watch. Then I scattered salt from my pack across the charred earth. Two pounds. Everything I had.

The fire was out before midnight. I drowned the coals. I checked every edge, every ember, every place where the heat might have crept beyond the boundaries I'd set.

The rage came back when I was checking the perimeter. Not the battle-rage — that was clean, simple, a thing with a purpose and a direction. This was the other kind. The old kind. The one that had risen in me when the farmers' torches licked at the Feywild canopy. The hot blind thing that doesn't care about right or wrong, that just wants something to *burn for what happened*.

I sat down on a rock at the edge of the blackened circle and put my hands on my knees and breathed.

In. Out. In. Out.

The cuts on my forearm and ribs and thigh throbbed in time with my heartbeat. I hadn't cleaned them. I should clean them. The Yuan-Ti used poison on their blades and I was sitting here bleeding into my trousers like an idiot while the malison was somewhere in the dark with his snake-arm and his calculated silence, already planning his next incursion, already forgetting the eladrin girl he'd killed as a footnote to an afternoon's work.

Khaziz. That was the name the halfblood had used, once, before I'd put him down. Hissed it like a prayer. *Khaziz will —*

Khaziz will nothing. Khaziz ran. Khaziz had an exit planned before the fighting started because Khaziz is the kind of thing that poisons a tree and a girl and a creek and walks away while someone else pays the cost.

I pressed my palms to the charred earth. Nothing. No root-whisper. No green memory. Just silence and salt and the ghost-smell of moonpetal.

I cleaned my wounds. I bandaged them with strips torn from my cloak. I picked up my axe and cleaned it too, methodically, the way I'd been taught — blade first, then haft, then the leather grip, wiping away blood that had gone dark and sticky in the hours since.

I did not sleep that night. I sat at the edge of Lirael's grove and I watched the dark and I waited for something — grief, rage, exhaustion, understanding — to tell me what to do next.

Nothing told me anything. The forest was quiet. Even the bird had stopped singing.

---

In the morning I buried her at the western edge of the grove, where the fire hadn't reached. I didn't have tools for a proper grave so I dug with my hands and a flat stone, and it took hours, and the soil was soft and dark and smelled like rain.

I didn't say anything over the grave. I'm not good at that. Words are for people who know what to do with them.

I left a bundle of fireweed on the mound. She used it in her poultices. She would have thought that was practical. She would have been right.

Then I walked south, toward the Serpent Hills, following the trail the malison's magic had left in the air — a thin, fading line of ozone and wrongness that my rage could follow the way a hound follows blood.

I didn't find him. I found signs of him — a camp, abandoned. A second poisoned grove, smaller, already dying. The scales of a shed skin near a river crossing that led back toward the material plane.

He'd gone back. Through the crossing, into the Serpent Hills, into the world of stone and open sky where an eight-foot Firbolg in a rage couldn't follow without being seen by every farmer and soldier between here and Waterdeep.

I stood at the crossing for a long time. The passage shimmered — still open, still breathing, not yet sick the way the later ones would become.

I could follow. Step through. Hunt him in his own territory.

The rage said *yes*. The rage said *burn him the way you burned her grove*. The rage said a lot of things, and all of them were simple, and all of them were wrong.

I turned back into the Feywild and walked north, toward the deep forest, toward the silence, toward the place where the trees still knew my name.

I didn't see Khaziz again for two years. But I remembered his face. The flat gold eyes. The calculated stillness. The way he'd said *interesting* while a girl bled out ten feet away from him.

Some things you carry in a bark case. Some things you carry in your jaw.

---

*[Lirael's grove grew back, eventually. Feywild soil is stubborn. But the yew never returned, and the moonpetal grows somewhere else now, and nobody talks to the new trees the way she did.]*
