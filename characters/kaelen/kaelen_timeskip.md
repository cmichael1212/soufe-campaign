# Kaelen "Connifer" Hazelheel

*Fragments from the edge of things. Not written — lived. These are the moments that stay.*

---

## The Clearing

*Year Five, late summer. The northern High Forest, near the foothills east of the Lost Peaks.*

---

The fire was wrong tonight.

Not the fire itself — that was fine, a small thing, barely knee-high, just enough to keep the chill off while the venison dried on its spit. He'd built it the way he always did: stones in a double ring, the inner gap packed with damp earth. Shallow pit, scraped to mineral soil, nothing that could catch. Dry hardwood, seasoned, the kind that burned clean and low and didn't throw sparks. No smoke worth mentioning. Invisible from fifty paces. A fire that knew how to be quiet.

Most people built fires carelessly. He'd seen it a hundred times — travellers kicking together a pile of whatever was dry, letting embers drift where they pleased, walking away in the morning without drowning the coals. It made his skin crawl in a way he'd stopped trying to explain. Fire was a tool, not a companion. You used it with respect and you watched it the entire time, the way you'd watch a blade laid on a table with the edge toward you. He kept a waterskin within arm's reach whenever a fire was lit. Always. The iron ring on his left hand — cold-forged, etched with a warding sigil he'd earned in the Feywild — helped. It dulled the bite of heat against his skin, turned a burn into a warmth, gave him the steadiness to work close to flame without flinching. But it didn't make him careless. Nothing would make him careless with fire. Not after the torches.

He still dreamed about them sometimes. The farmers stumbling through the Feywild crossing with their brands held high, firelight catching in the canopy, the ancient growth overhead curling away from the heat like fingers recoiling from a stove. The smell of singed moss. The sound of a young birch crackling as its bark split and wept sap into the flames. And the rage — his rage, rising up through his chest like a tide, hot and blind, and for one terrible moment he hadn't known if he would help the farmers or —

The fire in the pit popped softly. A bead of fat from the venison fell into the coals. He blinked and was here again.

No. The fire was fine. Everything else was wrong.

He'd felt it before sundown. A tightness across the back of his neck, below the hairline, where the skin is thin and the body knows things the mind hasn't caught up to yet. The birds had gone first — not all at once, not a startled flight, just... a thinning. One by one they stopped singing until the canopy was silent and the only sound was wind through the upper branches and the soft complaint of the fire.

Kaelen sat very still.

He was big enough that sitting still was an act of will. Nearly nine feet of Firbolg does not blend easily into a forest clearing, even one this deep, even at dusk. But he'd learned the trick of it long ago — not invisibility, not yet, just *stillness*. Becoming part of the landscape. A boulder. A stump. Something the eye slides over without catching.

The deer on the spit hissed and popped. A bead of fat fell into the coals.

He breathed in through his nose. Pine. Loam. Char. The green-copper smell of the creek thirty yards east where he'd filled the waterskin. And underneath all of it, faint, wrong — something acrid. Chemical. Like the air before lightning, but heavier. Wetter.

The hairs on his forearms rose.

He didn't look up. Not yet. That was the mistake prey made — jerking toward the threat, broadcasting their fear with motion. Instead he let his awareness expand outward the way Yewshade had taught him, years ago, in the deep groves where the Feywild's light bent sideways. Feel the forest. Let it tell you.

The trees were afraid.

Not the way trees fear fire or drought — slow, patient fears that build across seasons. This was immediate. A shudder running through root systems, a pulling-in of sap from the outermost branches. The oaks around his clearing were *cringing*. Ancient oaks, two hundred years or more, and they were contracting like children trying to be smaller.

Now he looked up.

The sky between the canopy gaps was the deep blue of late twilight, the first stars just finding their nerve. And across that sky, blotting out a river of stars as it passed, something moved. Vast. Silent. Black against the dark blue, visible only as an absence — a shape defined by what it ate.

It flew low enough that the wind of its passage stirred the upper canopy. Low enough that the acrid smell became a taste — copper and acid and something rotten underneath, the smell of a swamp compressed into a living thing. Low enough that Kaelen, sitting motionless by his quiet fire, could see the faint wet gleam of its underbelly as starlight caught the scales.

Wet iron. That's what it looked like. Wet iron, flexing.

The wingspan was wider than the clearing. The body between those wings was thick, muscled, *heavy* — this was not a creature that had to work to fly. It owned the air the way Kaelen owned the ground between root systems. Effortlessly. As though gravity had been informed of the arrangement and had agreed to cooperate.

A single drop of something fell from the darkness above. It struck a leaf ten feet from the fire with a soft hiss, and the leaf curled in on itself, edges blackening, dissolving into a wet nothing before it hit the ground.

Acid.

The dragon — and he knew what it was, had known the moment the trees flinched, had felt the name forming in some deep primal part of his brain that recognized predators the way roots recognize stone — the dragon passed over the clearing and continued south without slowing. It had not seen him, or had not cared. He was a mouse in a field and the hawk was hunting bigger things tonight.

Kaelen sat by his fire for a long time after the stars came back.

The venison had gone cold. He ate it anyway, tearing strips with his fingers, chewing mechanically, tasting nothing. His hands were steady. His breathing was even. But deep in his chest, in the place where the rage lived — the old rage, the one that had nearly consumed him when the farmers' fires licked at the Feywild's edge — something stirred. Not anger, not yet. Recognition.

The forest was quiet for the rest of the night. Even the creek seemed to whisper instead of babble.

When dawn came, gray and thin through the canopy, he broke camp and followed the dragon's path south. Two hundred yards from his clearing, he found the first trees. A stand of old-growth oak — bark stripped to pale pulp, wood soft and weeping, the ground beneath black and barren in a streak as wide as a wagon track. The acid had fallen in a line, casual, the way a dog marks territory without breaking stride.

He knelt and pressed his palm to the dead earth. Nothing. No root-whisper, no green memory. Just silence.

Something in his jaw tightened.

He followed the damage for half a mile before turning back. There would be more. There was always more, with something like this. The forest would tell him when it was ready.

## The Dwarf

*Year Five, early autumn. The foothills above Hammersong Hall.*

---

He smelled the dwarf before he saw him.

Iron. Boot leather. A particular pipe tobacco — fireweed and dried cherry bark, the kind the mountain folk favored. And underneath that, the harder smells: oiled chain, honed steel, the mineral dust that clings to anyone who lives inside stone.

Kaelen was in his half-elven shape, which he preferred in the lowlands. Six feet was tall for a half-elf but not remarkable — passable enough to avoid arrows and limit questions. He'd been tracking the acid damage south for tendays now — the dead stretches of forest were getting closer together, the poisoned creek beds more frequent. Whatever that thing was, it had claimed a territory the size of a small kingdom, and the heart of that territory was the mountain range ahead.

The dwarf was crouched behind a granite outcrop overlooking a river valley, scribbling in a leather journal with the focused intensity of someone who knew exactly what they were looking at and didn't like it. Short, broad, weathered in the way of dwarves who spent more time outside stone than in it. A scout. Older. Careful.

The dwarf had picked a good spot. Kaelen might not have noticed him at all if the wind hadn't shifted.

He could have passed by. Should have, maybe — the Firbolg Code said nothing about inserting yourself into other people's problems. *Give a thousand for nothing* didn't mean give it to everyone. Some silences were worth keeping.

But the river below was wrong. He could see it from here — the water had a faint opalescent sheen where it shouldn't, and the stones along the bank were pitted, eroded in patterns that didn't match natural weathering. The same damage. The same source.

The dwarf was watching the same river.

Kaelen let a branch crack underfoot. Deliberate. Loud enough to announce, not so loud as to threaten.

The dwarf spun with a hand on his axe haft and eyes like flint. Fast, for his age. Steady.

"Easy," Kaelen said, showing empty palms. Even in disguise, his voice was deeper than any half-elf's had a right to be. "I'm tracking the same thing you are."

The dwarf studied him for a long three seconds. The axe hand didn't move, but it didn't draw either.

"Big half-elf," the dwarf said flatly. "With Firbolg diction and oak bark on his belt." A pause. "I've heard stories about you. The farmers up north call you Connifer."

Not a question. An assessment. This was a dwarf who collected information the way Kaelen collected trail signs.

"Bronn Ironmantle," the dwarf said. "Steward of Hammersong Hall. And that river is *my* problem, so if you've got something useful to say, say it. If not, you're standing on my sightline."

Kaelen sat down. It took a while — even at nine feet, folding himself onto a rock ledge next to a dwarf required some negotiation with gravity.

"I found two hundred yards of dead oak, four days north. Bark stripped. Ground black. A flyover — she didn't even slow down."

Bronn's eyes changed. Not surprise — confirmation. The lines around his mouth deepened.

"She?"

"Black dragon. Saw her pass over my camp. Wingspan wider than this ridge." He paused. "The trees were afraid of her. Not the animals — the *trees*."

Bronn was quiet for a moment, which Kaelen appreciated. A dwarf who knew when to listen.

"That matches what we've got," Bronn said finally. He flipped the journal shut. "How long have you been tracking this?"

"The damage? Tendays. But the forest has been uneasy longer than that. Months. The crossings between here and the Feywild are... thinning. Destabilizing. Something is pushing on the boundaries, and it's not just the dragon."

Bronn gave him a look — not skeptical, exactly, but measuring. "Fey crossings. Planar boundaries. You talk like one of those Silverymoon academics, except you're the size of a barn door and you smell like a campfire."

"I talk like someone who walks between the planes and pays attention."

Another silence. Then Bronn nodded, once, and the axe hand finally moved — to his belt, where a waterskin hung.

"Water?"

"Thanks."

They sat together as the light shifted across the valley, and Bronn told him about the river. The acid contamination. The dead fish. The forge-water testing foul two months running. And underneath the practical concerns, something harder to name — a wrongness in the mountain's bones that even the stone-sense couldn't fully explain.

"I've been mapping the damage pattern," Bronn said, tapping the journal. "But I'm one dwarf with short legs and a territory that keeps getting bigger."

"I've been mapping it from the other direction," Kaelen said. "The forest side."

Bronn looked at him for a long moment. Then he closed the journal and said, "Show me."

---

They spent the next seven days in the foothills together, tracking the dragon's territory from both sides — Kaelen reading the forest damage, Bronn reading the stone. It was a strange partnership. The dwarf moved through terrain like water through cracks, efficient and low and impossibly quiet for someone carrying that much iron. Kaelen covered the canopy lines, the root damage, the places where acid had pooled in natural basins and killed everything for twenty feet in every direction. Between them, they built a picture.

The dragon — Bronn called her Nightscale, a name the Hall's scouts had given her — was expanding. Her territory had doubled since spring. The acid trails were fresher in the south, which meant she was pushing toward the lowlands, toward the trade roads, toward people who had no idea what was coming.

On the third night, sharing a fire Kaelen had built — Bronn had watched him construct it with the double-ring and damp earth and waterskin within reach and had said nothing, but his eyebrows had communicated approval — Bronn told him about Hammersong Hall. Forty-three people. A reclaimed kingdom. A king too young and too loud and too stubborn to know he was doing the impossible.

"His name's Ollum Hammersong," Bronn said, and the way he said it — plain, without ceremony, but with a weight that lived underneath the plainness — told Kaelen everything about what this dwarf thought of his king.

On the fifth day, Kaelen showed Bronn a fey crossing — or what was left of one. A ring of stones in a birch grove, the air between them flickering like heat haze, the passage unstable but still breathing. Bronn had stood at the threshold and gone very still, and Kaelen had watched the dwarf's face shift from skepticism to something closer to awe, and then to worry.

"This is connected," Bronn said. Not a question.

"I think so. The dragon, the crossings destabilizing — they're symptoms. Not the disease."

On the seventh morning, breaking camp for the last time, Bronn told him Ollum was in Neverwinter. Trade business, but also because the world was getting strange and the king knew it even if he wouldn't say so in those words. "He'd say something louder and less precise," Bronn added.

Kaelen turned the bark case on his belt. The metallic glint caught the morning light.

"Neverwinter," he said.

It wasn't the first time the name had surfaced. Twice in the last month he'd woken with the word in his mouth, pulled from dreams he couldn't hold onto — dreams of gold light on rooftops, of heat and ozone, of something vast and terrible pressing against the skin of the world. Yewshade had mentioned the coast, weeks ago, in the oblique way treants mention anything. *The roots reach further south than you think, sapling. Perhaps it is time you did as well.*

"I'm headed that direction," Kaelen said. Not a lie. Not quite the whole truth either.

Bronn studied him — but it was a different kind of study than the first day. A week of shared trail and shared fire had built something between them. Not friendship, exactly. Trust. The kind forged by watching how a person moves through the world when they think no one is measuring.

He reached into his pack and pulled out a folded square of parchment, sealed with a wax stamp Kaelen didn't recognize. The ink was fresh — Bronn had been writing it the night before, by firelight, while Kaelen pretended not to notice.

"If you find Ollum, give him this. It's an update on the situation here — the river, the sightings, the territorial pattern we mapped this week. He'll want current information."

Kaelen took the letter. It was heavier than it looked, or maybe that was just the weight of one man's trust in another.

"Tell him Bronn sent you and he'll hear you out. He hears out anyone, frankly — the dwarf has never met a stranger he didn't want to buy a drink. But if you mention the dragon, you'll have his full attention."

"And if I mention the planar instability?"

Bronn almost smiled. Almost. "Then he'll introduce you to a gnome with a telescope who'll talk your ears off about things you've *lived*. Might be worth the conversation."

Kaelen slid the letter into the bark case alongside the Firbolg Code. Two documents now. The old law and the new problem. They sat together like a question and its answer, neither one complete without the other.

"The king," Kaelen said. "The farmers up here — they've heard of him. A young dwarf who calls himself king of a lost hall."

"He doesn't just call himself that. He *is* that."

"I didn't say he wasn't." Kaelen stood, unfolding slowly, and Bronn's eyebrows rose slightly as the half-elven disguise didn't quite account for the shadow he cast. "But kings with forty-three people and a dragon at their door could use friends who know the forest."

"Could be," Bronn said. "Could also be that a Firbolg who walks between planes and tracks dragons alone could use friends who know how to hold stone."

Neither of them said anything for a moment. The valley below was sharp in the early light, the river cutting through it like a bright thread — even the poisoned stretches catching the sun.

"Safe roads, Connifer."

"Strong walls, Ironmantle."

Kaelen turned south. The forest opened ahead of him — beech and ash giving way to the older, wilder growth that ran toward the coast. He let the half-elven shape settle more firmly around his frame. Six feet of red-haired half-elf — or so the road would see — with a staff across his shoulders and a bark case at his belt, walking toward a city he'd only heard named in other people's stories and his own half-remembered dreams.

Behind him, Bronn was already writing again.

## The Road South

*Year Five, autumn into early winter. The Long Road, then the Sword Coast.*

---

He went back to the Feywild one last time.

After Bronn — after the week of shared trail and shared silence and the letter folded heavy in his bark case — he climbed north into the High Forest and found the crossing near the old birch grove. The one he'd used a hundred times. The air between the stones shimmered, thinner than he remembered, but it held. He stepped through.

The Feywild welcomed him the way it always did — a deepening of color, a sharpening of scent, the feeling of a held breath releasing. The forest on this side was older and stranger and more alive, and for a few hours he was home in a way that the material plane could never quite match.

He found Yewshade in the deep grove. The treant was diminished — bark grayer than Kaelen remembered, crown thinner, the great root system that had once hummed with the steady pulse of centuries now stuttering, skipping beats like a heart losing its rhythm. The grove around Yewshade was failing. Young trees curled inward, leaves spotted and brittle. The air smelled wrong — dry where it should have been damp, still where it should have been breathing.

Yewshade's words came slow and heavy, the way they always did, rising through the earth and settling in Kaelen's chest: *Go south, sapling. The wind carries a wrong scent, and the roots beneath the coast are humming in a key I do not recognize. Find others. You cannot stand alone against what is coming, and you were never meant to.*

Not a suggestion. Yewshade's suggestions were never suggestions.

Kaelen left the grove walking south. He found the crossing at the edge of the old wood — the one that opened onto the material plane near the Long Road, the one he'd used on a dozen trips between worlds. It was flickering. The passage that had always been a doorway was now a window, and the window was closing.

He stepped through.

Behind him, the crossing sealed shut. Not a slow fade, not a seasonal thinning — a severance. The sound of it was wrong — a deep, percussive silence, like a door slamming in a house with no doors. The air where the passage had been went flat and dead, and the connection he'd felt his entire life — that constant low hum of the Feywild breathing on the other side of the world's skin — went silent.

He stood on the material plane side with his hand pressed to the bark of the nearest tree and felt nothing. No shimmer. No hum. Just wood and silence and the ordinary wind.

The crossing was gone. The Feywild was gone. For the first time in eighty-five years, he was standing in the only world he had, and the door behind him was bricked shut.

Something tightened in his chest that was not grief and was not rage and was both.

He turned south and started walking.

---

The visions came more often as he moved south.

They weren't dreams, exactly. Dreams had structure — a beginning, a logic, even when the logic was strange. These were *fragments*. Shards of something broken, catching the light at odd angles.

Gold on rooftops. A thin line of amber burning along a horizon he'd never seen.

Heat. Ozone. The taste of lightning without the storm.

A sound like glass cracking, except the glass was the sky, and the cracks ran in every direction, and through the cracks he could see —

He'd wake with his hands gripping the earth, fingers dug into loam or sand or road-dust, heart slamming, the rage coiled tight in his chest like a snake surprised from sleep. The images would fade inside a breath, leaving only the physical: sweat on his temples, tension in his jaw, a ringing in his ears that took minutes to resolve into ordinary silence.

Something was wrong with the world. Not the forest-wrong of Nightscale's territory, not the slow decay of destabilized crossings. Something bigger. Something that pressed against the fabric of every plane at once, like a hand pushing through a curtain, testing for the weakest point.

The road south was long. He'd walked stretches of it before, between crossings, on the brief forays into the material plane that had built his quiet reputation along the forest's edge. But this time there were no crossings to duck back into. No Feywild rest stops. Just the road, and the weight of the sky, and the silence where the hum used to be.

He passed through settlements in his half-elven disguise. A night at an inn near Longsaddle where the barmaid looked at him sideways and said, "You're the tallest half-elf I've ever seen." He tipped well and left before dawn. Two days sheltering from a storm in a woodcutter's lean-to outside Conyberry, sharing dried fish with an old woman who asked no questions and talked only about her cats. A brief, grim afternoon near the edge of Neverwinter Wood where a fey crossing he'd used before — a reliable one, a moss-ringed arch between two ancient ash trees — was already dead when he arrived. No shimmer. No hum. Just two old trees standing over a patch of air that had forgotten what it used to be.

He pressed his palm to the bark of the nearest ash and listened. The tree was confused. Frightened in the slow, deep way that trees experience fear. Its roots reached toward the place where the crossing had been and found the same nothing he'd felt at the one behind him. A wall where there had been a window. That made two. He didn't want to know the number by the time he reached Neverwinter.

And then the bark case at his belt grew warm.

Not the warmth of sunlight or friction. A living warmth, like pressing your hand against the flank of a sleeping animal. The metallic glint — the one he'd noticed for years without ever thinking to question — brightened, shifting from a faint shimmer to a steady green-gold pulse that he could feel through the leather of his belt.

Kaelen unlatched the case with careful fingers. Inside, the Firbolg Code scroll sat where it always had. But around it — threaded through the bark walls of the case itself, emerging from the wood as though they'd been growing there for years, waiting — three saplings. Small. Pale. Root-threads as fine as spider silk, woven in a lattice of loam that smelled like the deep Feywild, like rain on ancient moss, like home. They pulsed with the same green-gold light as the case, faintly, in a rhythm that wasn't quite a heartbeat but wasn't quite *not* one either.

The message came through the earth.

Not words — Yewshade didn't use words the way people did. It rose through the ground into the soles of his feet and climbed his bones like sap climbing heartwood, slow and heavy and ancient, and when it reached his chest it settled there with a weight that was not weight, and he understood.

*The grove is failing, sapling. The roots cannot hold where the soil between worlds is being pulled away. These three are the youngest — the only ones small enough to move. They need a place where the boundaries are not walls but bridges. I cannot choose that place. You must. You have walked more worlds than I have roots. Trust what you have seen. Trust what you will see. Plant them where the planes can breathe.*

The case had always been the vessel. Yewshade's gift, years ago, offered with a treant's patience and a treant's long planning — the bark, the metallic glint, the careful crafting that Kaelen had taken for simple generosity. It had been waiting. Dormant. Ready. And when the last crossing within reach sealed itself shut and the grove's need became urgent enough to wake what slept inside, the case had opened like a seed pod in its season.

The Firbolg Code and the saplings lay together in the case. The old law and something new. He closed the latch gently, feeling the warmth through the bark, and stood for a long time with his hand resting on it while the ash trees creaked overhead in a wind that no longer carried the Feywild's scent.

Three lives. Three small, impossible, fragile lives, and a message from a treant who could not follow, and a world that was closing its doors one by one.

He put Bronn's letter back and kept walking. The case was heavier now, or maybe that was just the weight of knowing what it carried.

## The City

*Year Six, month of Uktar. Neverwinter.*

---

He did not like cities.

This was not new information. He had never liked cities, in the same way he had never liked shoes or ceilings or conversations that lasted longer than the subject warranted. Cities were *compressed* — too many lives in too little space, all of them loud, all of them leaking emotion and noise and smell into a shared atmosphere that battered his senses the way surf batters a cliff face. Constant. Relentless. Unnatural.

Neverwinter was worse than most, because Neverwinter was also *broken*. He'd heard the stories — the eruption, the cataclysm, the decades of rebuilding — but stories hadn't prepared him for the reality of a city that was still, on some fundamental level, in pain. The streets were clean and the buildings were repaired and the people moved through their routines with the practiced normalcy of survivors, but underneath it, in the places where stone met earth and earth met root and root met whatever deep, slow processes gave a place its character, Neverwinter *ached*. An old wound, mostly healed, still tender when pressed.

He'd entered through the eastern gate at dawn, disguised, hood up, staff across his shoulders. At six feet, in the half-elven shape, he could pass. The guards barely glanced at him — just another tall traveller with road dust on his cloak. Neverwinter had seen stranger things. But underneath the illusion his shoulders ached from holding themselves narrower than they wanted to be, and the city pressed in from every side.

The Driftwood Tavern was easy enough to find — Bronn's instructions had been precise, which was the most Bronn thing imaginable. *East of the market square, near the old wall. Looks like it washed ashore and decided to stay. You'll hear the dwarf before you see the building.*

Bronn had been right about that, too.

Even from the street, through a closed door and warped timber walls, the sound of a voice — loud, musical, full of a warmth that had nothing to do with the fireplace — carried into the cold morning air like a herald announcing that someone inside was *alive* in a way that most people never managed.

Kaelen stood outside for a moment, the bark case warm at his belt. His hand drifted to it and rested there — on the green-gold pulse of Yewshade's bark, on the Firbolg Code and the three saplings nested beside it, on the sealed words of a pragmatic dwarf who trusted his king enough to send a stranger to find him. Three lives and an old law and a letter. Everything he carried, in one case.

The visions had been worse last night. Gold on rooftops. Glass cracking. Something pressing through.

He pushed open the door and stepped inside.
