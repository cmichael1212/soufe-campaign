# The Journal of Thulduran Gleamshadow, Illusionist

*Selected entries, spanning five years following the conclusion of the Stone Tooth campaign*

---

## 7th of Flamerule, Year One

I have returned to Silverymoon.

The journey from the Sword Coast was unpleasantly muggy, but uneventful, which suited me fine. After the business with the orcs and the citadel and all those *people* requiring explanations for things they ought to have understood themselves, I welcomed the silence. Ollum offered to accompany me as far as Everlund, singing the entire way, naturally. I declined, though not unkindly. The dwarf means well. He simply hasn't yet grasped that not every moment requires a soundtrack.

Selanlar's estate is as I left it. Dust on the reading tables. Her half-finished cup of blackmoss tea still sits on the windowsill in the study — I cannot bring myself to move it. The university's Bursar, a tedious man named Aldenmere, met me at the gate with documents. After three years missing, the Convocation has agreed to grant me stewardship of the property and its contents "in trust, pending resolution of Professor Selanlar's status." A deliberately temporary phrase. They want me to know this is borrowed.

I signed the papers without argument. I am not here for the house. I am here for her library.

## 18th of Nightal, Year One

Deepwinter approaches and the excitement in the streets — lanterns strung across the bridges and sugared almonds sold at extortionate prices in the market square — seems to fill everyone with energy and anticipation.

I see only tiresome distractions.

By contrast, the University Library is quiet and, since the estate ran short of firewood (a temporary problem of no consequence and in need of no elaboration), also where my thoughts currently find their clearest form. As such, each afternoon is a pleasant routine: Astronomy first, then Planar treatises, then — in a moment of professional charity — the shelves on Construct Theory. The institution's holdings are vast, respectable, and thorough.

And outdated.

I borrowed twelve volumes on Astral Motion three tendays ago and returned them yesterday, annotated. Their authors persist in treating celestial drift as a closed system. It is not closed. It is interconnected. If one assumes permeability and overlap — even minor permeability — the mathematics simplify enormously. I wrote this in the margins. At length.

The librarian frowned at the density of my corrections but said nothing. She reveals herself a sensible woman.

If the solstice fireworks are particularly obnoxious tomorrow night, I may remain in the stacks until dawn.

*Post script:* Twice now I have noticed the same human student lingering two tables over while I work. He pretends to read but watches my diagrams instead. Today I overheard him whisper to his companion that I "rewrote Halvarn's Third Postulate in an afternoon."

I did not rewrite it. I corrected it.

## 22nd of Hammer, Year Two

I have begun cross-referencing the star charts I acquired during our travels with the observatory logs kept by the University's Department of Celestial Studies. The department head, a sun elf named Vaelithra, was courteous enough when granting me access, though she made a point of noting that "guest privileges are reviewed annually." I thanked her and refrained from mentioning that her department's cataloguing system contains no fewer than fourteen redundancies.

Restraint is a virtue I am cultivating.

The charts are promising. Several notations in *Meridian's Treatise on Astral Currents* align with observations I made through the telescope during our time in the field. But the alignments are imprecise — no doubt Meridian's equipment was inadequate. They are close enough to suggest a pattern but too loose to confirm one. I need better data. I need to observe more consistently, from a fixed position, over months.

Fortunately, I appear to have nowhere else to be.

## 4th of Alturiak, Year Two

Rhaugilath shared the following earlier this afternoon after I returned another set of corrected texts: *"An alarming number of these books were written by people who did not finish their thoughts."*

"You cannot possibly know that," I told him.

*"I can,"* he replied, *"because they stopped writing immediately before the interesting part."*

I was lost for response.

He has begun directing me toward particular shelves when we enter the stacks. Ignoring these suggestions has revealed itself perilously inefficient; twice now the book has led me directly to texts containing obscure references relevant to my research that would have taken me ages to find on my own.

When asked how he knows where these things are, he claims he can "hear the pages thinking."

This is both quite absurd, and unfortunately, the most revealing answer on the subject yet offered.

## 3rd of Mirtul, Year Two

Selanlar's personal library is extraordinary. Three hundred and twelve volumes I have catalogued so far, and I suspect another hundred remain in storage cases neatly arranged in the cellar. Inexplicably, through some as-yet unpenetrated mechanism of delightfully mundane magic, they remain pristine, dust free, and entirely without wear or damage. She truly was a marvel.

*[several lines appear to have been written, crossed out, then somehow magically erased, though the signs remain]*

Her collection on Illusory Theory surpasses anything the University holds publicly, which I suspect they know. I surmise that to be the actual reason they granted me stewardship rather than seizing the estate outright.

I found a pressed flower between the pages of her copy of *Mordenkainen's Luminous Geometries*. Silverbell. She always kept them on her desk.

*[several more lines again appear to have been written, crossed out, and magically erased, this time accompanied by water damage or signs of dried liquid on the pages]*

I have not opened that book again.

## 14th of Eleint, Year Two

A frustrating realization: the work I need to do next cannot be done here. Silverymoon excels for Theory and consulting the corpus of any of several fields.

What I require now, however, is a *Forge*. Tools, Materials, Experimentation, and the space to fail loudly and without complaint from the neighbors.

After some inquiry, I have secured a lease on a modest workshop in Waterdeep's Trades Ward. The rent is offensive — one could fund a small expedition for what they charge per tenday — but the space is adequate. High ceilings, a reinforced workbench, and neighbors who are apparently accustomed to occasional explosions. The landlord, a dragonborn woman named Krath, asked only that I "keep the fires inside the building." I assured her I would endeavor to do so.

I will split my time between cities. Research in Silverymoon. Construction in Waterdeep. It is inefficient, but necessity makes pragmatists of us all.

## 1st of Nightal, Year Two

A breakthrough, or the ghost of one.

For months I have been conducting parallel observations: tracking the lit points on my stone against known stellar positions using the University's ephemeris tables. The correlation should be straightforward — if the points represent stars, they should match... but they do not. Not a single point on the stone corresponds to a catalogued star, planet, or object.

At first I assumed error and recalibrated. I checked my arithmetic twice — then a third time out of spite toward the numbers themselves — no error.

The points on the stone are not tracking celestial bodies. They are tracking something *else*. Something that moves in relation to the stars but is not of them. The rotation I observed years ago is not stellar drift. It is... I do not yet know what it is. But it is *real*, it is *measurable*, and it is the most exciting data I have encountered since I first looked through the telescope.

I may have shouted. The librarian gave me a look. I gave her one back.

The student who comes to observe my investigations seemed impressed by my defiance, and I was, in turn, surprised to find his response somewhat satisfying.

## 19th of Ches, Year Three

I have named him Skitter.

He is, objectively, not a success. His left foreleg seizes every forty seconds with metronomic reliability. He cannot climb surfaces steeper than thirty degrees without tumbling backward in a manner that would be comedic if it were not so costly to repair. His visual lens fogs in humidity, which in Waterdeep means it fogs *constantly*. And he has developed an inexplicable fixation on copper coins — I found seven of them stuffed into his thorax cavity last tenday, which I did not instruct him to do and cannot account for.

And yet, he moves. He responds to basic commands. He navigates a room, avoids obstacles — most obstacles — and returns to me when called. He is a mechanical spider approximately the size of a housecat, built from brass and iron and a dozen enchantments I had to invent because the existing literature on construct animation is, frankly, embarrassing.

He is my first, and is deeply flawed... but I am unreasonably proud of him.

Krath saw him skittering across the workshop floor and did not even flinch. "Seen worse," she said. I could not completely restrain my smile.

## 27th of Ches, Year Three

Dumbstruck does not even come close.

Skitter has strangely continued gathering worthless trinkets: small metal objects, copper coins, nail heads, once a spoon from the tavern across the street. At first I assumed a fault and paid it no mind — he includes several rudimentary "collect" routines for laboratory cleanup.

But today, I found eight coins arranged beneath the workbench in a configuration that undeniably showed comprehension and iteration upon one of my planar coordinate diagrams.

Skitter possesses neither the processing matrix nor the thaumaturgical architecture to reason. I simply hybridized a casting of "Unseen Servant" and "Animate Objects", with some minor integration linkage. And yet, it is as if somehow 2 and 2 have made 5.

I sat in stunned silence for several minutes, my mind whirling with the possibilities: independent thought and tactical reasoning?!

> **STEADY NOW, GLEAMSHADOW**
>
> *[this line only is written in an annoyingly perfect and old-fashioned script, distinct from the rest of the journal entry and clearly not of Thulduran's hand]*

*Post. Script.* I hate when Rhaugilath writes in my journal.

## 27th of Tarsakh, Year Three

The last 2 tendays... or was it 3?... have been a whirlwind. I can't remember the last time I saw my bedchamber. All other research has halted.

The research has coalesced to four parallel avenues: the ideas come thick, fast, unbidden, and almost fully formed — I've never felt more alive. Or exhausted. I need sleep.

**Locomotion & Articulation**
Framework: synthesized from the essential threads of "Animate Objects" and simple servitor layers from "Unseen Servant"; Skitter remains the primary testbed: joint tolerance, balance under asymmetrical load, recovery from destabilization.

**Directed Cognition** *(a generous term)*
Early results: astounding, inexplicable, anomalous, unrepeatable — one trial (Skitter) — defies all explanation or replication in subsequent attempts; "Mordenkainen's Faithful Hound" bears more scrutiny. Frustration: immense.

**Concealment & Presence Suppression**
If the construct is to function as intended, it must not simply be hard to see. Illusion will be necessary, but insufficient.

**Material Substrate**
The current material, indeed all standard metals, woods, stones, or gemstones, are wrong — entirely wrong. They are loud, bright, reflective, brittle, weak, heavy, and entirely unfit. I require something different: subtle... unnoticed.

## 18th of Mirtul, Year Three

The month of Tarsakh was a heady but exhausting blur. Reviewing the notes I see I've embarked upon something extraordinary, but that will take time.

Returning to my astral researches, and recalling the obstacle I ran up against, I realized why the detour was so easy.

Were this a lesser man's journal, no doubt they would actually write the word "sigh" at this point. I, of course, shall do no such thing.

Instead, I have swallowed my pride and written to Ollum.

The cradle design exists in my head — I can see it, a lattice of silver wire woven into a concave frame to seat the stone at the telescope's focal point. The geometry is sound. The enchantments are interlaced elegantly, if I say so myself, which I do. The problem is that my hands are those of a scholar, not a smith. Six attempts at working the silver wire have produced six misshapen disasters that would not hold a grape, let alone channel planar energy. The wire must be drawn to a precise and uniform gauge thinner than spider silk, and the weave tolerances are measured in fractions of a hair's width. This is not a task for enthusiasm and good intentions. This is master-level metalwork.

Ollum Hammersong — King of a reclaimed dwarven hall, dual-wielder of legendary weapons, and the only person I know who can forge metal with the precision this requires — also happens to be a friend. So I wrote to him. I described the technical requirements in detail, included diagrams, and asked if he might spare a tenday to visit Waterdeep and assist.

His reply arrived in four days. It read, in its entirety: "On my way. Don't touch the silver until I get there. — O."

Efficient. Surprisingly so, for a man who once took forty-five minutes to tell a barmaid about his great-great-great-grandfather.

## 2nd of Kythorn, Year Three

Ollum has been in Waterdeep for six days now and I must confess two things.

First, the dwarf's skill at the forge is *extraordinary*. I do not say this lightly. I have studied artificer techniques across eleven reference texts and observed the work of three so-called master craftsmen in the Trades Ward. None of them — not one — could do what Ollum did on his second day here. He examined my diagrams for perhaps ten minutes, asked two questions (both annoyingly insightful), heated a crucible, and drew silver wire so fine and uniform that I had to use a magnification cantrip to confirm it was real. "It's just metal, Thully," he said, as though he had not just accomplished in an afternoon what I had failed to do in three tendays. He calls me "Thully." I have asked him to stop. He has not stopped.

Day by day, the lattice frame is taking shape. He works the wire with a delicacy that seems impossible for fingers that thick, humming some dwarven forge-song the entire time. Always humming. Always singing. The man cannot exist in silence for more than thirty seconds — I have timed it. When he is not singing or humming, he is telling stories to Krath, my landlord, who appears genuinely entertained. When he is not telling stories to Krath, he is telling stories to the barmaid at the tavern across the street. When he is not telling stories to the barmaid, he is *leaving with* the barmaid, returning the next morning smelling of ale and perfume and looking enormously pleased with himself.

"You should get out more, Thully," he told me this morning, still wearing yesterday's shirt. "There's a whole city out there."

"I am aware of the city, Ollum. I rent a workshop in it."

"That's not what I meant and you know it."

I do know what he means. I simply have no interest in spending my evenings the way he spends his. Some of us grapple with the cosmos' most unfathomable questions, testing our wits against the very mysteries of nature itself.

Second confession: I am genuinely glad he is here. The workshop feels less like a workspace and more like... I don't know... something warmer. He brought a cask of ale from Hammersong Hall and insists we drink a cup each evening while he subjects me to the day's new ballad or saga or limerick. They are, occasionally, not terrible.

I will not tell him that.

## 6th of Kythorn, Year Three

The cradle is finished.

Ollum left yesterday morning, after a final inspection of the frame that involved holding it up to the light, squinting, tapping it once with a fingernail, and declaring "Aye, she'll hold." As farewells go, he also subjected me to a rib-crushing embrace and made me promise to visit Hammersong Hall before the year was out. I promised. I may even keep it.

The design is a lattice of silver wire — Ollum's exasperatingly perfect wire — woven into a concave frame that seats the stone at the precise focal point of the telescope's primary lens. The metalwork is his. The enchantment is mine. I spent two days inscribing each strand individually, channeling the stone's emanations into the optical path without diffusing them. My hands still ache.

Tonight, for the first time, I observed through the telescope with the stone seated in its cradle.

The sky was *different*.

At four points along the northern horizon, where my naked eye and the unaugmented telescope show nothing but darkness between stars, I saw — shimmers. Faint, iridescent distortions, like heat rising from summer stone but vertical, hanging in the sky. They pulsed slowly, not in unison but in a rhythm I could almost feel in my teeth.

I do not know what they are. I believe — I suspect — they are points where the boundary between our plane and another grows thin. Closed doorways, perhaps, long since sealed, scarred, and forgotten. Or more troublingly, the future locations of new passageways...

I observed for four hours. I filled eleven pages with sketches and measurements. My hands were shaking by the end, and not from the cold of the cradle stabilization magics.

I am close to something. I can feel it.

## 9th of Flamerule, Year Three

Rhaugilath occasionally asks questions about the stone, or the cradle, or the new observations — annoyingly impenetrable ones.

*"Have you considered,"* he said this evening while I adjusted the telescope lens, *"that the points you are observing might not represent locations?"*

"What else would they represent?" I asked.

*"Decisions."*

I scoffed.

*"Many things make them, you know,"* he said mildly.

I dislike it when the book speaks in riddles.

He does it more often when he is certain he is correct.

## 11th of Eleasis, Year Three

Waterdeep is intolerably humid. The workshop walls sweat. I am considering minor ventilation enchantments.

More interesting: I returned to Silverymoon for three days to consult a restricted index in the celestial archives. When I arrived, I found a small stack of books already waiting at my usual table — texts on illusory permanence, planar resonance, and an obscure Chondathan manuscript on spectral filtration.

I had not requested them, but they were, to my surprise, exactly what I was looking for. The human student's furtive glances from his table across the room immediately laid bare the "mystery" of their origin. I smirked and got to work.

At mid-morning, the lad from Deepwinter — Corvin, is his name — approached and asked, with painful earnestness, whether my "permeability correction" implied that stellar bodies might serve as anchor points for transitive planar shear.

The question was somewhat clumsy and clearly practiced, but neither foolish nor possible without rigorous consideration.

I answered him. Had I known what was to follow, I'm not sure I wouldn't have chosen a different approach.

Nonetheless, word spreads quickly in academic halls starved for novelty. By afternoon, there were four of them, and six for the evening meal. They asked questions tirelessly, occasionally contradicting one another, occasionally surprising me. One of them — a moon elf woman named Saeril — had independently derived a variant of my own lattice stabilization concept, though her symmetry assumptions were flawed.

When I rose to leave, they were still arguing excitedly over a diagram I had sketched.

It is... efficient. If they chase down minor proofs and eliminate dead ends, I need not waste time doing so myself.

I will return in a tenday. There is a filtration theorem I would like tested.

## 2nd of Marpenoth, Year Three

A letter from Ollum arrived today.

He writes that Hammer's Song has begun behaving... strangely.

The weapon apparently refused to be wielded by another dwarf during a recent skirmish — growing cold to the touch and nearly leaping from the man's hands. Ollum also claims the hammer has begun communicating impressions to him: warmth, displeasure, anticipation of battle.

Sentient weapons are not unheard of. They are, however, rarely cooperative research subjects.

Ollum asks whether I might examine it.

This will require delicacy. A weapon that recognizes its wielder may not appreciate interference by a gnome with instruments.

Still, the opportunity to study such a construct is rare and invaluable.

I have begun drafting a small illusion matrix that may persuade the hammer it is still being held by its rightful owner: convincing a thinking object is less about deception than courtesy.

I shall send for him once I have worked out all the details.

## 17th of Kythorn, Year Four

My preparations took longer than anticipated, but Ollum journeyed to Waterdeep at my request, and arrived carrying Hammer's Song wrapped in a wool blanket as though it were an infant.

The hammer did not approve of my first attempt at examination.

The illusion lattice — designed to project a simulacrum of Ollum's arcane signature around my hands — lasted approximately six seconds before the weapon threatened all my laboratory's glassware with a low vibration strong enough to trigger cracking sounds across the room. Needless to say, I stopped immediately.

"She's suspicious," Ollum said, with evident pride. "Well, I'm just getting started," I retorted.

The second attempt was more successful, if still conducted with a low thrum permeating absolutely everything.

By layering the illusion with a minor sympathetic link to Ollum himself — essentially borrowing the dwarf's magical "accent," as Rhaugilath put it — I was able to hold the hammer long enough to perform a basic resonance reading.

The sensation is... difficult to describe. Hammer's Song contains a presence: old, patient, not exactly hostile, but extremely particular about who is permitted to touch it.

When I returned the weapon to Ollum the vibration once again halted immediately.

Just before I did so, however, I felt a faint warmth from the pommel in my hand.

Ollum insists that means she "likes" me.

I suspect the truth is more complicated.

## 3rd of Eleasis, Year Four

The group has acquired a name: "The Gleamshadow Circle."

Appalling.

I objected.

They ignored me.

There are nine of them now, including, unexpectedly, Professor Ilyth Dorran from Applied Thaumaturgy. Dorran claims she attends only because "the Convocation has grown timid" — a diplomatic phrasing for what she later described, over tea, as intellectual cowardice.

The Gatherings are irregular and unofficial. They seem satisfied with whatever spare time I may have or odd hours I might suggest. We gather in unused lecture rooms, in the observatory after hours, once in my own study at Selanlar's estate — though that will not become habit. They bring proofs. I dismantle most of them. Occasionally I do not.

Saeril has refined her lattice theory considerably. Corvin has begun constructing a crude prism array for isolating planar frequencies — simple, but promising. When he showed it to me, hands shaking slightly, I felt an unfamiliar hesitation before I would have normally pointed out its fatal flaw: instead, I softened the phrasing.

Professor Thandril passed us in the corridor yesterday evening. He looked from Dorran to the students to me, and his expression was... assessing. I have since learned he petitioned the Convocation for stricter oversight of "unsanctioned instructional assemblies."

If he believes nine curious minds constitute a threat to the university, then perhaps they do.

Decaying institutions calcify what they touch.

## 12th of Eleasis, Year Four

During last week's gathering, I offered as a small point of interest a brief recording and demonstration of the resonance structure experiment that permitted the analysis of Ollum's hammer several months previous.

I thought it would be a mild curiosity, but the Circle's reaction was rather amusing.

Corvin stared at the spectral trace for nearly ten minutes before quietly asking whether the harmonic pattern implied the weapon possessed an independent will.

Saeril began listing the currently-accepted "wisdom" on autonomous construct theory and sentient weapon arcana that conclude there is no theoretical backing to support such a procedure, and that sentient weapons are opaque to all arcane probes.

With her eyes wide with shock, Professor Dorran said in a whisper, *"Good gods."*

I still don't see what all the fuss is about.

## 21st of Eleasis, Year Four

I met Bronn Ironmantle today.

Ollum's steward is a practical man — the sort who listens for five minutes, asks three questions, and then produces a ledger demonstrating precisely why your idea will either succeed or fail.

We spoke regarding Trollskull Manor, the tavern our party technically owns in Waterdeep but has collectively ignored for years.

Bronn believes the establishment is underperforming.

I believe the establishment is an opportunity. And tallying the books lately has revealed my personal research coffers, as well as the University stipend, are dwindling quickly.

Between his logistical competence and my modest ability to influence perception, we may be able to increase its profitability considerably. Ollum's role in this venture will consist primarily of appearing occasionally, singing loudly, and convincing entire rooms of strangers that they are having the greatest evening of their lives.

This is, admittedly, a skill set.

We will begin with small improvements, Bronn will manage the books, and I will see what can be done about the atmosphere.

Damn this city and damn its politics.

A Professor Thandril — mentioned once in passing in a prior entry, with whom I have had only passing interactions, and (most importantly) has published precisely *nothing* of note in the last decade, I checked — has filed another petition with the Convocation to "reassess the stewardship arrangement" regarding Selanlar's estate. His "argument," if his drivel can be called such, amounts to the claim that the university's property interests are "inadequately served by a non-affiliated resident of uncertain academic standing."

*Uncertain academic standing.*

I have FORGOTTEN more about illusory theory than this man has read.
I have built a functional automaton.
I have broken new ground on sympathetic emotional illusion lattices that GENERATIONS of researchers will spend their WHOLE LIVES studying!

I have made BREAKTHROUGHS that his ENTIRE department could NOT achieve even if they had FIFTY YEARS to attempt to repeat what I've done IF I TOLD THEM HOW TO DO IT!

*[several additional densely-punctuated and fully capitalized lines are evident but also thoroughly scratched out; the entry continues on the following page]*

I am not faculty.

## 2nd of Uktar, Year Four

I received word from Bronn, summarizing the first month of our endeavor, precisely when he said he would report. Ollum doesn't know how lucky he is.

*"Trollskull Manor has experienced a modest increase in patrons and revenue over the previous 3 tendays."*

Bronn personally attributes this to improved procurement of ale, and included Ollum's assessment that the cause was his performance five tendays ago (paraphrased, I'm sure).

My own explanation is in the success of the illusion I installed above the hearth — a subtle projection of drifting lanternlight and phantom music that gently improves the mood of anyone within thirty feet.

All three explanations may be true, but I'm satisfied with our first attempt: an unexpected windfall of gold pieces.

Per our previously-agreed sequence, we'll proceed to phase 2 and look outside Trollskull.

## 9th of Uktar, Year Four

I found Selanlar's notebooks.

Not her academic publications — those I have read many times. These are personal research journals, hidden in a false panel behind the cellar wine rack. Seven leather-bound volumes in her precise, elegant script. She was methodical, as always. She dated every entry.

She was studying planar convergences.

The same phenomena. The same shimmers I can barely resolve through my telescope — she was theorizing about them years before I ever looked through a lens. Her mathematics are elegant, her observations meticulous. She had mapped provisional locations for what she called "thinning points" along the Sword Coast, and she was developing a theoretical framework for predicting when they might open.

I'm still at a loss to explain exactly how she came by her data: I've not yet discovered any equipment in her Manor that might have facilitated such observations.

Her final volume ends mid-sentence. The last dated entry is three days before she disappeared.

I sat in the cellar for a very long time after reading that.

She was ahead of me. She was *always* ahead of me. And whatever she found — whatever she saw or calculated or understood that I have not yet reached, it took her somewhere. Or something took her because of it.

I will not stop. If this path leads to where she went, then it leads to her.

## 12th of Uktar, Year Four

In the last few days, Bronn and I executed the next step in our "strategic hospitality initiative."

A failing tavern in the Dock Ward changed hands quietly — several minority stakes were secured through shell partnerships — Bronn really does have an excellent head for business.

Tonight Ollum performed there for the first time.

The usuals presumably expected nothing of note, perhaps only cheap pisswater in a forgettable tavern soon to shut its doors forever.

Instead they experienced a dwarf shouting battle ballads to stir the soul, wake the dead, and break the heart, beneath illusions of the slaying of dragons and the triumph of heroes.

Patrons began arriving from neighboring streets before the second song ended, and by the third, there were fights to get through the door.

Bronn's positive report was, as ever, understated.

I suspect this plan just might work.

## 15th of Uktar, Year Four

While reviewing Selanlar's notebooks tonight I asked Rhaugilath a direct question.

"Did you know her?"

The book was silent for several minutes.

Finally he replied, *"I have known many wizards."*

"That was not my question."

*"No,"* he agreed.

He then turned several pages by himself and refused to elaborate further.

I am beginning to suspect Rhaugilath knows more about the path Selanlar followed than he is willing to say.

If so, I will eventually persuade him to speak.

Books, like people, have their pressure points.

## 15th of Tarsakh, Year Five

Skitter lost a leg again. The left foreleg — always the left foreleg. I have rebuilt that joint nine times now.

I have come to suspect the problem is not mechanical but enchantmental; the animation matrix simply does not distribute load evenly across asymmetric limb structures.

It may also be the Ogre's fault, angered as it was by Skitter's successful theft of a Wand of Magic Missiles from the monstrosity's no-doubt stench-filled warren. Setting aside how such a valuable item ever found its way into the brute's possession, in retrospect, the left foreleg is a small price to pay.

That aside, I need better references on force distribution than anything in Silverymoon or Waterdeep can provide.

This also confirms what I have started to suspect: locomotion, cognition, and survivability cannot be solved independently. In real-world scenarios, a construct that can move, but not think, dies. A construct that can think, but not hide, is targeted. A construct that can do both, but is made of the wrong material, is simply destroyed more efficiently.

Which leads to the fourth problem, that must also be solved in coordination: the material.

But conventional alloys have been found wanting: what could possibly serve?

I also find myself confounded of late analyzing my nightly cosmological observations: specifically, my telescope appears to be lacking... something.

I can see the shimmers through the eyepiece. I can measure their positions and correlate them, roughly, with the stone's point-map.

But I cannot *resolve* them. I cannot tell what lies beyond them nor can I differentiate one planar signature from another.

It is as though I am standing before a row of locked doors and though I can see the doors, I cannot see the rooms behind them, cannot read the signs above them, nor even determine if they are doors at all or merely paint on the wall.

I need a lens. A filter. Something that can separate planar light into its constituent frequencies the way a prism separates white light into color.

Such a thing must exist — the theory demands it.

But if anyone on the Sword Coast has built one, they have not written about it, and if they have written about it, they have not written about it in any language I can read, and I can read eleven.

Selanlar's notebooks end where my understanding ends. She knew what came next but did not write it down, or wrote it down and took it with her.

## 25th of Tarsakh, Year Five

An alchemical curiosity has occupied all my thoughts.

In a back stall of the Waterdeep market I purchased a fragment of what the merchant claimed was "shadowglass." The substance is neither glass nor shadow, but something between: a thin black plate that refuses to fully occupy the same space as the table upon which it rests. Viewed from the side it appears almost absent.

Upon initial investigation and a recording of its properties, I realized I had previously come across oblique references in three separate texts — each instance incomplete, dismissive, and clearly written by someone who had no idea what they were dealing with: "Umbral distillate," "Night's residue," "the skin of a shadow."

Shortly thereafter, Rhaugilath pointed me toward an unremarkable figurine on a forgotten shelf in a hot and musty antique shop — my cup runneth over.

His preferred name is apparently "Velarith." I think all these fanciful names are pointlessly imaginative and distracting; we may as well call it "Gleamshadow," for all the sense that makes.

Initial results are extraordinary.

It can be solidified, or it can be poured and folded, and it adheres to surfaces but does not seem to possess volume, no matter how "deep" or "thick" the application is.

Whether the liquid form is applied to a metal plate, or the solidified form is shaped into one, the plate becomes... less present: blurred edges and refracted light.

When illuminated directly it darkens. When placed in shadow it nearly disappears. And when left in shadow unattended, the coated object occasionally appears in some *other* shadow elsewhere in the room.

And it was only after several days of trials and tests that I noticed my primary reservoir had not lost volume. Somehow, the coating process has not been "using up" my supply of material.

Rhaugilath described it as *"a poor man's attempt at condensed umbral matter,"* which is the sort of comment that is either deeply insightful or deeply pretentious. With him it is often both. He also flatly refused to explain why I didn't seem to be consuming at least a few mL's with each trial. I'm starting to believe he simply *can't* explain it — I certainly can't.

This might be the perfect outer layer for an outer chassis: a construct that could move *through* shadow rather than merely stand in it would be difficult to target.

And very difficult to destroy.

This will require further experimentation.

## 2nd of Kythorn, Year Five

Thandril's petition advances. The Convocation has scheduled a "review hearing" for the autumn. Aldenmere delivered the notice personally, with what I can only describe as performative sympathy. "I'm sure it will all work out," he said, in the tone of a man who is sure of nothing and cares about less.

Let them have their hearing. If they take the estate, they take it. The wine rack is empty — the notebooks were removed months ago — and Selanlar's books are already catalogued in my memory: every page, every line, every word, and all 4 typos. They can have the shelves.

Though I've grown quite fond of my circle of pupils, I am growing tired of libraries that have shown me everything they have to show, and markets that sell variations of things I have already bought, and scholars who study the edges of the map but never venture past them.

The answers I seek are not here. And though progress in some avenues is possible, I suspect change is brewing.

The telescope shows me doors I cannot open. The stone shows me a map I cannot fully read. Selanlar followed this same path and it led her beyond the reach of anyone who stayed behind.

I do not know where to go. But I have occupations until I determine the next steps.

In the meanwhile, Skitter sits on the corner of my desk, his one working eye-lens tracking my quill as I write. His left foreleg twitches. I should fix it.

## 11th of Eleint, Year Five

The noteworthy contents of the laboratory include the following:

One partially articulated brass chassis (Skitter), three failed limb assemblies (two dissolved), a planar aperture containment circle re-etched after the first attempt proved insufficient, a shallow but seemingly inexhaustible basin of "the substance" and an ever-increasing quantity of coated samples, the (triumphant) remains of Whisper's scrapped chassis as well as the painstakingly and pristinely restored animation matrix.

And some loot. We'll get to that.

With all interior and exterior components fully coated, "Silence" spells engraved on approximately 2 out of every 5 (the rest of the spells I know affixed to the rest), the name was chosen for obvious reasons. It required a significant refinement of the coating process, an enormous amount of time and resources, and several new "blended arcane constructions" which the textbooks insist aren't stable (or possible).

I beg to differ.

He is (was?... will be, again) capable of both quadrupedal and bipedal movement, as fast as a horse in full gallop, but in almost complete silence, and with instructions unnoticed at all times.

He is also capable of completely phasing into and moving from shadow to shadow, provided speed is kept moderate. And after years of research, and months of preparation, I sent him to steal a Tarrasque egg.

It was, of course, unsuccessful: I realize now they don't lay eggs.

Nevertheless, the results speak for themselves: I sent him alone, through the planar aperture, to infiltrate a barren hellscape where the Tarrasque reigns supreme, with instructions to steal something from its lair.

He returned, battered, broken, on the brink of collapse, dragging himself through the aperture by... loyalty? mission? robotic instruction sets? sheer force of will?... alone, and collapsed on the other side. As the aperture shrunk to nothing (automatically), I became aware of 2 things: a faint, terrifying roar that shook my bones and rattled every object in the lab, and the light in Whisper's eyes going out.

I will keep to myself which left the greater impact.

Inside the "portable compartment" enchantment I created and affixed to his chest, I would later find several items:

- a massive tarrasque tooth, nearly the length of a forearm, reddish brown darkening toward black at the root, with metallic veining like streaks of lightning visible on the sides
- a quartz monocle filled with moving starlight
- a black iron compass
- a blackwood wand
- a brooch

The infiltration was not without cost, but was undeniably a success. I have ideas already, and much work is needed to iterate on the previous design...

Rhaugilath has been unusually attentive, encouraging, and even helpful throughout these experiments. And it's not lost on me that he's now guided me toward two of the caches of Gleamshadow (and its derivatives) that I've found...

At one point he said, very quietly:

*"Gleamshadow, you are approaching a solution that several people spent entire lifetimes wisely avoiding."*

"That is because they were insufficiently motivated," I replied.

*"Yes,"* he said. *"That is always the reason."*
